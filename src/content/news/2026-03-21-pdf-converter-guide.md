Converting files to and from PDF is one of the most searched-for document tasks in Australia. Whether you need a Word to PDF converter for a final submission, a JPG to PDF converter for scanned images, or a way to extract editable text from a PDF back into Word or Excel, the right tool depends on the direction of conversion and the quality you need.

This guide covers the most common PDF conversion scenarios with practical recommendations for both free and paid options.

## Creating PDFs from Other Formats

### Word to PDF

The simplest Word to PDF converter is already on your computer. In Microsoft Word, go to File, Save As, and select PDF. This produces a clean, well-formatted PDF from any Word document and preserves formatting, images, and hyperlinks.

For batch conversion -- converting multiple Word documents to PDF at once -- you need dedicated software. Tungsten Power PDF supports batch Word to PDF conversion, as does Adobe Acrobat Pro.

Free online Word to PDF converters like Smallpdf and iLovePDF work for one-off conversions but require uploading your documents to their servers.

### JPG to PDF and PNG to PDF

Converting images to PDF is common for archiving scanned documents, photographs, or design proofs. Most PDF editors handle this natively -- open the images and save as PDF.

For a quick free JPG to PDF converter or PNG to PDF converter, online tools like iLovePDF and Smallpdf let you upload multiple images and combine them into a single PDF with page order control.

On Mac, Preview can convert images to PDF by opening the image and exporting as PDF. On Windows, the built-in Print to PDF function works for individual images.

If you are converting scanned document images to searchable PDF, you will want OCR (optical character recognition) applied during conversion. See our [OCR text recognition guide](/news/2026-03-21-ocr-text-recognition-scanned-pdfs) for details.

### Excel, PowerPoint, and Other Formats

Microsoft Excel and PowerPoint both include built-in Save As PDF functionality. For more control over the output -- such as selecting specific sheets, adjusting page layout, or combining multiple files into one PDF -- use a dedicated PDF creator like Power PDF or Adobe Acrobat.

## Extracting Content from PDFs

### PDF to Word

Converting PDF to Word is trickier than the reverse because PDF is a fixed-layout format while Word is a flow-layout format. The quality of conversion depends heavily on how the original PDF was created.

For PDFs created from Word or other text applications, Tungsten Power PDF and Adobe Acrobat both produce good results that preserve formatting, tables, and images. Power PDF uses its own conversion engine and handles complex layouts well.

Free PDF to Word converters exist online, but the output quality is inconsistent -- particularly with tables, columns, and embedded images. For important documents, use desktop software.

### PDF to JPG and PDF to PNG

Extracting pages from a PDF as images is useful for presentations, social media, and web content. Most PDF editors include export-to-image functionality. You can typically choose resolution, colour space, and which pages to export.

Free online tools handle basic PDF to JPG conversion adequately for non-sensitive documents.

### PDF to Excel

Extracting tabular data from PDF into Excel is one of the most frustrating conversion tasks. Tables in PDF do not have underlying structure -- they are just positioned text and lines. Quality varies dramatically depending on the source.

Power PDF Advanced and Adobe Acrobat Pro both include PDF to Excel conversion. For best results, use OCR if the PDF is scanned, and expect to do some manual cleanup of complex tables.

## Choosing the Right PDF Converter

For occasional one-off conversions, built-in tools (Save As PDF in Office) and free online converters cover most needs. For regular business use -- particularly batch conversion, high-quality extraction, and sensitive documents that should not be uploaded to third-party servers -- a desktop PDF editor is the practical choice.
